Why Platform Engineering Is Suddenly the Talk of the Tech World?

Everywhere you look, conversations about platform engineering are gaining momentum. From industry conferences to LinkedIn threads, it seems everyone is talking about building platforms to power the next wave of digital transformation. But why now? And what makes platform engineering more than just a fleeting trend?

Over the past two decades of working in investment banking and FinTech, I’ve witnessed firsthand how technology evolves to meet the growing demands of businesses and users. One consistent challenge has been balancing speed with reliability and ensuring security while fostering innovation.

Why Platforms?
Platforms are more than just a trendy buzzword- they have become the cornerstone of modern engineering as they solve fundamental problems that many organizations face:

Reducing Cognitive Load: Platforms abstract repetitive and complex tasks, allowing product teams to focus on delivering features and innovation rather than wrestling with infrastructure.
Improving Reliability: Dedicated platform teams ensure that shared capabilities—such as CI/CD pipelines, observability tools, and security frameworks—are resilient and expertly managed.
Accelerating Delivery: Shared tools and best practices mean teams don’t have to reinvent the wheel, leading to faster time-to-market.
Minimizing Risk: Centralized governance of platform capabilities ensures compliance with security, regulatory, and functional standards.
Optimizing Costs: Platforms enable teams to leverage public cloud services efficiently while maintaining control over the user experience.

Reflecting on my experience, I’ve seen how the absence of a platform-led approach often results in duplication of effort, slower delivery cycles, and a fragmented user experience. Platforms provide the much-needed structure to address these gaps.

As Martin Fowler aptly puts it, “A platform’s goal is to reduce the cognitive load of application development teams, enabling them to ship better code faster.”
What Defines a Platform?
At its heart, a platform is a collection of tools, services, and processes that enable application developers to work more efficiently. It’s the bridge between infrastructure and application teams, designed to:

Abstract repetitive tasks like deployments and scaling.
Enforce guardrails for security and compliance.
Offer a unified developer experience that empowers teams to innovate.

Platforms create consistency across teams without stifling autonomy, a concept that aligns with Atlassian’s insight: “A platform’s value lies in its ability to create consistency while allowing teams to retain their autonomy.”
In industries like fintech and many others, where precision and speed are non-negotiable, platforms serve as a structured approach to building resilient, compliant, and high-performing systems. They offer businesses the ability to centralize capabilities like development, testing, and deployment while ensuring consistency across teams.

What Sets Great Platforms Apart?
Successful platforms share key traits:

Platform as a Product: Platforms should be treated like customer-facing products. Their success depends on how well they meet the needs of their users. This means constant collaboration with app teams and iterative improvements based on feedback.
User Experience Focus: Platforms thrive when they are easy to use, with clear interfaces and intuitive workflows.
Self-Service: Teams should be empowered to access resources independently, reducing bottlenecks.
Comprehensive Documentation: Onboarding should be smooth, supported by clear and actionable documentation.
Modularity: A platform should offer services that can be used independently, catering to diverse team needs.
Secure by Design: Security must be embedded into every aspect of the platform to protect organizational assets.
Internal Developer Portals: Tools like Backstage provide centralized hubs for managing services, enhancing discoverability and promoting standardization.

Spotify’s “Golden Path” templates and Netflix’s “Freedom and Responsibility” model are prime examples of how platforms empower developers with autonomy while maintaining consistency. Platforms like these don’t just support development—they transform it.
Lessons From Spotify and Netflix: Platform Excellence in Action
Platform engineering is not merely a technical framework; it’s a cultural and operational shift. It empowers teams to focus on delivering value by simplifying workflows and fostering collaboration. The success stories of companies like Spotify and Netflix illustrate this vividly.

Spotify's Golden Path: Spotify introduced predefined workflows, or “Golden Paths,” that guide developers toward best practices while still allowing flexibility. These templates empower teams to build quickly and confidently, knowing they’re aligned with company-wide standards.
Netflix’s Freedom and Responsibility Model: Netflix pairs autonomy with accountability, enabling teams to innovate while adhering to platform guardrails. This balance ensures creativity thrives without compromising consistency or reliability.
Both companies demonstrate that a well-designed platform is not just about tools—it’s about creating an ecosystem where teams can succeed without unnecessary friction.

Platform Capabilities: The Building Blocks of Success
The capabilities of a platform define its value. These capabilities not only simplify development but also enforce consistency, governance, and efficiency. Capabilities may comprise several features, meaning aspects or attributes of the parent capability’s domain.

Here are capability domains to consider when building platforms:

Web portals/MFEs for observing and provisioning products and capabilities
APIs (Domain Driven APIs) for automatically provisioning products and capabilities
“Golden path” templates and docs enabling optimal use of capabilities in products
Automation for building and testing services and products
Automation for delivering and verifying services and products
Development environments such as hosted IDEs and remote connection tools
Observability for services and products using instrumentation and dashboards, including observation of functionality, performance and costs
Infrastructure services including compute runtimes, programmable networks, and block and volume storage
Data services including databases, caches, and object stores
Messaging and event services including brokers, queues, streaming and event fabrics
Identity and secret management services such as service and user identity and authorization, certificate and key issuance, and static secret storage
Security services including static analysis of code and artifacts, runtime analysis, and policy enforcement
Artifact storage including storage of container image and language-specific packages, custom binaries and libraries, and source code
By aligning these capabilities with the needs of developers and product teams, platforms unlock unparalleled productivity and innovation.

Challenges in Platform Adoption
Implementing platforms isn’t without its hurdles:

Treat Platforms as Products: The success of a platform is tied to the success of its users. Platform teams must partner closely with app teams to co-create features and gather continuous feedback. Mandating adoption without collaboration often leads to resistance.
Prioritizing the Right Capabilities: Start with frequently required and undifferentiated capabilities like pipelines, databases, or observability tools. Working with a few engaged teams initially can help refine the platform and create internal champions.
Securing Leadership Buy-In: In large enterprises, leadership may view platforms as cost centers rather than strategic enablers. Platform teams must demonstrate their direct impact on business outcomes, aligning their goals with product value streams.

As Martin Fowler points out: "The biggest challenge of platform engineering is not the technology but changing how teams think about ownership, collaboration, and shared goals."
From my own career, I’ve learned the importance of building trust between platform teams and application teams. Early collaboration ensures that platforms are seen as enablers, not gatekeepers.

Measuring Platform Success
To gauge the effectiveness of a platform, organizations should measure:

User Satisfaction: Surveys and Net Promoter Scores (NPS) provide insights into developer experience.
Productivity Gains: Frameworks like SPACE measure the effectiveness of platform tools in enabling developers.
Operational Efficiency: Metrics like time-to-provision services and deployment latency reflect platform efficiency.
Business Impact: DORA metrics as Deployment frequency, lead time for changes, Time to restore services after failure and Change failure rate other key metrics highlight the platform’s contribution to business goals.

The Path to Platform
Platform engineering is no longer a buzzword; it’s a strategic necessity. By treating platforms as products, focusing on user experience, and fostering a culture of collaboration, organizations can unlock new levels of efficiency and innovation. As we look to the future, platforms will play a pivotal role in how technology teams deliver value in an increasingly complex world.

From my years of experience in the trenches, I can say this with confidence: Platforms are not just about technology—they’re about enabling people. And in enabling people, we create the foundation for sustained growth and innovation.

The real question is: How will your organization harness the power of platforms to redefine its future?
